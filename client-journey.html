Step 03

Lead Qualification

This is where you decide if the lead is worth pursuing. Quick internal review, then change the lead status to "Qualified" to trigger the next step.

Internal OMG Booth reviews
Review & Qualify the Lead
A new lead hits your VSCO Workspace inbox. Open it, review their event type, date, guest count, and description. Ask yourself: is this a real opportunity or a tire-kicker? This should take 2-3 minutes per lead.

Qualification Checklist

  • Has a real event date? (or at least a timeframe)
  • Budget aligns? Guest count and event type suggest they can afford your packages
  • Serviceable area? Atlanta metro or within your travel range
  • Not a duplicate? Check if this contact already exists in your system
  • Responsive? If they left a phone number, a quick call can speed things up

What to Do

If qualified: Change lead status to "Qualified" in VSCO Workspace. This auto-triggers Form 2 (Event Details).

If not a fit: Change status to "Not Qualified" or "Closed". Send a polite decline email or don't respond.

If you need more info: Call or email them directly. Don't change the status yet.

Suggested "Not a Fit" Email

Hey {{primary_contact.first_name}}, thanks for thinking of OMG Booth! After reviewing your event, we don't think we're the best fit for what you're looking for right now. If anything changes, we're always here. Wishing you an amazing event!

Critical setup: The "Qualified" lead status does not exist in your VSCO Workspace yet. You must create it at Settings › Lead Sources › Lead Statuses before any of this works. This is blocker #1 in the overhaul plan.

Some operators avoid this question. Don't. A client whose budget is under $1,200 is not going to book a $2,800 package. Finding this out before you spend time building a proposal saves everyone's time. Frame it as "so we can recommend the right package" not "can you afford us."

Step 11

Review Gate

The final client-facing touchpoint. Triggered automatically by the Post-Event Report (Form 6) data. If the event went well, ask for a Google review. If it didn't, ask for private feedback. No guessing, no blanket review blasts.

Automation Triggered by Form 6 data
Smart Review Routing
When the Post-Event Report (Form 6) is submitted, the automation reads two fields: Event Rating (1-5 scale) and Guest Engagement (Low / Medium / High). Based on those answers, the system routes the client into one of two paths. No manual decision needed.

Routing Logic

  • Happy Path: Staff rated event 4-5 AND engagement marked "High" → send personalized Google Review request (email + SMS)
  • Concern Path: Staff flagged issues OR rated event 3 or below → send private check-in email asking for feedback (email + SMS, no Google link)

VSCO Automation Config

Observable: Questionnaire Completed (Form 6: Post-Event Report)

Condition A (Happy Path): Event Rating ≥ 4 AND Guest Engagement = "High" → Action: Send "Google Review Request" email template + SMS template

Condition B (Concern Path): Event Rating ≤ 3 OR any issue flags present → Action: Send "Private Feedback Request" email template + SMS template

Timing: Fires immediately when Form 6 is submitted (typically 1 day after the event). The email lands while the experience is still fresh.

Evaluation

Do You Need All 6 Forms?

Here's the honest assessment. Every form should earn its place. If it doesn't collect information that changes what you do, cut it.

FormVerdictCould You Skip It?What You'd Lose
Form 1: Lead Intake Essential No. This is your front door. Leads would have to email or call. You'd lose the auto-create and auto-response.
Form 2: Event Details Essential No. You need this to build proposals. You'd be guessing at event scope, doing calls for every lead, and sending generic quotes.
Form 3: Event Logistics Essential No. Embedded in quote — zero extra effort. Your team shows up without venue details, load-in times, or on-site contacts.
Form 4: Design Brief Important Maybe — for social/casual events where you use a default overlay. You'd be chasing logos and brand colors via email threads. Works for corporate, essential for brand activations.
Form 5: Pre-Event Report Important Maybe — if you have a very small team and do verbal check-ins. No written record of event readiness. Works until someone forgets the printer or shows up at the wrong venue.
Form 6: Post-Event Report Important Maybe — but you'd lose your feedback loop. No data on what happened at events. No way to track quality, identify recurring issues, or coach team members.
Bottom line: Forms 1-3 are non-negotiable — they're the booking engine. Forms 4-6 are about quality and professionalism. You could operate without them for simple events, but they're what separate a photo booth operator from a premium experience company. At OMG Booth's price point ($1,800-$4,200), clients expect this level of organization.

The Real Question

The old system had 28 questionnaires because every scenario got its own form. The new system has 6 because every form is universal — sections with clear headers replace conditional variants. The client only fills out what's relevant, guided by instructions rather than forced by separate form links.

Is 6 the right number? Test it. If Form 4 (Design Brief) consistently goes unfilled for social events, make it conditional — only trigger the automation for Corporate and Brand Activation job types. The automation system supports this: just add a Job Type condition to the trigger.
